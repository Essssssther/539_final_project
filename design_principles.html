Core Design Principles

The Road's Trio: Safety, Cognitive Processes, and Pleasure of Driving

Driver factors account for 90-95% of traffic accidents, often due to a mismatch between driving requirements and the driver's abilities, influenced by the complex "driver-vehicle-road" environment and variability in information processing.

Safety

Driver-related issues are involved in 90-95% of traffic accidents, primarily due to a gap between what driving demands and what the driver can handle.


Safety is the issue that lays the foundation for all design. Whether it's the three-second principle mentioned above or any other design principle, it's all about maximizing the safety of the driver and the passengers in the vehicle.

Core Design Principles

Cognitive Processes

For a driver's cognitive process, two types of information are essential:

•External, comprising road and vehicle conditions gathered from instrumentation, measurements, and observations

•Internal, referring to the driver's feelings, perceptions, performance, thinking, judgment, emotions, and sensations.

From a traffic ergonomics perspective, key cognitive driving behaviors include perception, intentional decision-making, and control.

Core Design Principles

Pleasure of Driving

Every driving behavior carries a degree of risk, but in actual driving, drivers assess this risk subjectively, often struggling to recognize the objective danger.


When a driver's perceived danger exceeds the actual risk, they tend to drive cautiously, a behavior termed "safe," while the converse leads to riskier driving; this perceived risk is measured against an axis known as the "individual acceptable danger."

Extra Credit Design Concepts

Enhancing the Journey with Innovations in User Experience

Emotional design in automobiles, representing the highest level of the user experience, is receiving increasing attention and focus from designers.

Scenario-Based Design

The system discerns users' personalized needs in different contexts, offering them the most relevant interactive content or services, moving away from the traditional multi-level search mode dependent on manual input.


This approach minimizes user decision fatigue, with intelligent recommendations complementing user control, ensuring they remain central to the product experience.

Extra Credit Design Concepts

Ambient Design

Humans naturally exchange information, concepts, and emotions with objects and other humans through various sensory dimensions, with our sensory systems interacting in multiple ways.


For instance, ambient lighting (visual), fragrance (olfactory), seat adjustments (tactile), and voice systems (auditory) all contribute to a personalized emotional experience through different senses.

Extra Credit Design Concepts

Emotional Design

Visual emotional design creates a resonant emotional connection with the user through an innovative integration of digital color, texture, and dynamics in the cockpit.


This approach offers direct emotional feedback, fostering a sense of immersive context and enabling a more comfortable and enjoyable psychological experience in the cockpit.
